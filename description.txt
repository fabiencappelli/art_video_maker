Niamh (pronounced Neev) is one of the most well known women of Irish mythology. Her name means “brightness” or “radiance”, and she is often called Niamh Chinn Óir, “Niamh of the Golden Hair”.

She appears in the tale Oisín in Tír na nÓg, written down in medieval manuscripts around the 12th century, though the story itself likely lived in oral tradition long before that.

In the legend, Niamh is the daughter of Manannán mac Lir, the sea god and keeper of the Otherworld. One day she rides across the sea on a white horse and arrives in Ireland, where she meets Oisín, the son of the warrior and poet Fionn mac Cumhaill, leader of the Fianna.

They fall in love, and Niamh invites him to leave Ireland and come with her to Tír na nÓg, the “Land of Youth”. Riding across the waves, they reach a place where no one grows old, illness does not exist, and joy never fades.

Oisín lives there with Niamh for what feels like three years. But eventually he begins to miss Ireland and the Fianna. Niamh lets him return on her horse, with one warning: he must never touch the ground of Ireland.

When Oisín arrives back, he discovers that centuries have passed. The Fianna are gone, the world has changed, and when he accidentally falls from the horse, the years catch up with him all at once. His grave is sometimes said to lie at Glenalmond in County Antrim.

The story became deeply woven into Irish storytelling and landscape. Places connected to the Fianna, such as the Hill of Allen in County Kildare, are often linked to Oisín’s story, and many coastal traditions imagine the Otherworld lying somewhere beyond the western horizon.

Niamh belongs to that horizon. She is the one who arrives from beyond the sea, the one who invites the crossing into another world.
